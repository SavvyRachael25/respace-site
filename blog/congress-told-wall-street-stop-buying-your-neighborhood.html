Congress Just Told Wall Street to Stop Buying Your Neighborhood

reSpace Team · Mar 23, 2026 · 8 min read
Article Image

The 21st Century ROAD to Housing Act passed the House 390 to 9. It cleared a Senate procedural vote 89 to 9. The message from Washington is not subtle.

Institutional investors controlling 350 or more single-family homes will be banned from purchasing more. This is not a partisan talking point. This is bipartisan legislation with near-unanimous support. It validates something a growing number of Americans already believe. Homes should belong to people. Not hedge funds.

What the ROAD Act actually does

The Reducing Opportunities for Acquisition and Development Act, the ROAD Act, targets the largest institutional buyers. Firms that control 350 or more single-family homes would be prohibited from purchasing additional properties. The goal is direct: reduce the competition between people who want to live somewhere and funds that want to profit from it.

This is not a small symbolic gesture. Institutional investors have purchased hundreds of thousands of homes since 2012, converting owner-occupied neighborhoods into portfolios. In some zip codes, they account for a significant share of all purchases. The ROAD Act draws a line.

The culture is already shifting

The legislation did not come out of nowhere. It reflects a cultural moment that has been building for years. Renters who watched their landlords get bought out by private equity. First-time buyers who lost bidding wars to all-cash institutional offers. Neighborhoods that changed not because people moved but because ownership changed hands at scale.

The frustration is real and it is bipartisan. The vote count proves it. When the House passes anything 390 to 9, that is not a close call. That is a mandate.

Homes should belong to people. Not hedge funds. Congress just made that official.

What this means for Seattle

Seattle has not been immune. The Pacific Northwest has seen significant institutional activity in its surrounding submarkets. Tacoma, Renton, Kent, and Federal Way have all experienced waves of investor acquisition. These purchases compress inventory and drive up prices for everyone else trying to buy.

If the ROAD Act is signed into law, the effect on Seattle's market would be gradual but meaningful. Fewer institutional buyers competing for inventory means more opportunity for the people who actually want to live here. That is a structural change, not just a policy statement.

Where co-homeownership fits

reSpace was built for a market where the traditional path to ownership had been systematically closed off. Institutional investment was one of the forces doing the closing. But even with the ROAD Act, the core problem remains: home prices in Seattle's most desirable neighborhoods are still well beyond the reach of most individual buyers on their own.

That is the problem co-homeownership solves. Not by waiting for the market to correct. Not by moving to a cheaper city. By restructuring how ownership works so that more people can access it in the neighborhoods they actually want to live in.

The ROAD Act is an acknowledgment that the old model was broken. Co-homeownership is a new model that works now.

The math Congress just validated

Here is what the ROAD Act implicitly confirms: single-family homes are a wealth-building asset that belongs in the hands of residents, not institutions. Every vote for that legislation was a vote that said homeownership matters. That access to it matters. That the systematic removal of inventory from individual buyers has real consequences.

That is exactly the thesis reSpace is built on. Ownership builds wealth. Wealth changes lives. The structure that makes ownership possible should serve the person living in the home, not a portfolio manager in another city.
